## Summary Translation (Flowing English)

**Internal Affairs**

The following matter concerns His Majesty our Padishah, Protector of Islam, possessed of majesty, power, and grandeur. With his benevolent intention of caring for his subjects, this is the special article describing the imperial journey to the Dardanelles and Edirne that took place several months ago, with which this newspaper has been auspiciously inaugurated.

His Majesty our Sovereign of fine conduct is characterized by his care for his subjects. Although news and the science of geography have informed His Imperial Majesty to some extent, following the principle that "hearing is not like seeing," [His Majesty resolved] to observe with his own eyes—both the Muslim and non-Muslim subjects' conditions, and the fortifications and state of the imperial fortresses—with complete compassion. As an auspicious and unprecedented beginning, with humble condescension, His Majesty chose to burden his precious imperial person with travel, and to announce and demonstrate his inherent compassion as a sovereign to the poor.

Departing from Istanbul on the twenty-second of the noble month of Dhu'l-Hijja 1246 [May 22, 1831], which fell on a Friday, after performing the Friday prayer, His Majesty boarded the frigate named Şeref-resan [Honor-Bestowing] and departed for the Dardanelles with the Imperial Fleet. The fleet consisted of three ships-of-the-line, four frigates, four corvettes, one steamship, and two cutters—totaling fifteen vessels.

His Majesty stayed six days in Gallipoli and performed the Friday prayer at the Gazi Süleyman Pasha Mosque, led by the Second Imam of his royal retinue, Mustafa Nuri Efendi, one of the senior professors. After the prayer, he departed with the fleet for Kale-i Sultaniye [Çanakkale]. After one week, leaving the Imperial Fleet at the strait, His Majesty traveled by steamship [back toward Edirne].

On the sixth of Muharram [1247], a Friday, after again performing the prayer, His Majesty returned to Gallipoli. On the seventh of the month, departing at dawn, he proceeded overland to Oreşe, Keşan, and Cisr-i Ergene, staying at the notables' mansions designated for his lodging. At the farm named Kara Kasım, three hours from Edirne, His Majesty spent one night.

On the eleventh [of Muharram], a Wednesday, His Majesty arrived at a place half an hour from Edirne. From the Solak Fountain, with a grand procession arranged in his honor, he proceeded toward the Selimiye Mosque. In its vicinity, at the mansion of the Grand Vizier's sword-bearer Raşid Ağa—which is new and pleasant and was designated as the imperial palace—His Majesty deigned to honor [with his presence].

From the mentioned fountain, the Molla of Edirne and all the ulema and sheikhs prepared for the imperial reception. The schoolteachers with all their pupils, and the Greek, Armenian, and Jewish priests and rabbis with their children, wearing their ceremonial garments, stood along the royal road, raising their voices in prayer and praise. All the people lined up along the roads with complete joy, and by observing the imperial countenance, they were gratified.

From Gallipoli to the protected city of Edirne, the deputies, notables, and leading figures of the lands that His Majesty inspected came from places half an hour away, gratefully and thankfully welcoming the royal arrival. Each official was honored by being clothed in ferâce and kukuleta robes of honor. In each place, however many poor Muslims and non-Muslim subjects there were, they were registered and abundant alms were given on their behalf—they being recipients of the most complete imperial favor.
