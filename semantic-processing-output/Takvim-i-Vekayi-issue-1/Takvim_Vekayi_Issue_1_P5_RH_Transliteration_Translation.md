## Summary Translation (Flowing English)

### Section 1: Conclusion of the Military Demonstration (Continued from P4 LH)

Following behind, when the battalion imam completed his prayer according to the rule, the customary signal [was given]. With this, the officers and military enlisted men, chanting three times "My Padishah, long live!" with their murmur, acclaimed [His Majesty]. After their acclaiming, the mentioned battalion executed: "Battalion half-right wheel! Battalion forward! Double file forward! Close! Road! Man wheel!" With these commands, they were passed over the bridge. Afterwards, the command "Shoulder arms!" was given.

Since the other battalions were found on the side of the first battalion, there was consideration that perhaps difficulty would occur in executing the ceremony of servitude [salute] in order. While the mentioned first battalion was executing the ceremony, the other battalions executed "half-left" and, marching, entered the column in their direction. In that manner, at the moment the first battalion completed the execution of the ceremony, the tenth regiment's battalion, and following it the twelfth [regiment's] Military Supplies Regiment battalion, and behind it the seventh regiment's battalion, and after that the ninth regiment's battalion—consecutively, after moving like the first battalion—at the place named Servirlik, all of them were formed into battalion in battle line.

Afterwards, for their passage to the Capital, with the attachment of the imperial will, the Commander-in-Chief Pasha His Excellency—with their medals designated to their ranks, at the very front, exceeding fifty order sergeants being armed; and thereabouts the present regimental commanders, deputies, and regiment superintendents, two by two; and more than a hundred guards, marching in two rows; and behind them in row, a complete music band consisting of one hundred forty persons—with this adorned regiment, according to regulation, accompanying the mentioned battalions via Üsküdar's market street, they descended to the Great Pier. With the officials' arrangement, embarking onto the boats that had been prepared and readied, they transferred the Victorious Army to the Istanbul side.

And while the mentioned regiment was crossing, whether from the guilds and populace, or from the spectator groups found on the road—small and big—altogether, the movements causing the execution of the necessary ceremonies of respect and honor—they fulfilled them with complete desire and enthusiasm.

### Section 2: Fire Prevention Measures

Based on the consecutive emergence of partial and total fires due to the requirement of fate, whether for extinguishing or for protection and vigilance from the causes of fire—in the Capital, the three lands [Galata, Üsküdar, Eyüp], and the Bosphorus, every person was instructed to guard their neighborhoods at night with lit lamps, turn by turn. This was commanded, and in that manner, for about twenty days, the neighborhoods and, with the officials' supervision, the state warehouses were guarded, and its effects were also generally observed.

Now, seriously and again seriously, since no need remained—relying on eternal [divine] protection—permission was given for everyone to rest.

### Section 3: Imperial Inspection of the Gunpowder Works (27 Rebiülâhir 1247 / October 5, 1831)

The gunpowder manufactured in the Imperial Gunpowder Works—every year, with the pillars of state and its superintendent present, rotation, arrival, and storing is customary. This time also, its time having arrived, it was reported to the blessed imperial dust-foot [the Sultan] from the Commander-in-Chief Pasha His Excellency's side, and a petition and permission was sought.

Since sublime attention to this kind of necessary matters is evident, with desire for personally honoring the works, on the twenty-seventh of Rebiülâhir [October 5, 1831], Tuesday, by sea, His Majesty went to Yedikule Pier, and from there departed by imperial carriage. The gunpowders' strength, quality, and quantity were viewed through test-firing of cannons and bombs. And their unparalleled effort—from this aspect also there was proclamation; and to the servants and workers, an abundant imperial gift was favored.

And for one night, His Majesty stayed overnight at Lehistanoz Pavilion. The next day, after the meal, the imperial person was in their carriages, and the intimates of the sultanate in other carriages, being thus arranged, from inside Istanbul, they went to the New Imperial Palace (Topkapı), and from there returned by sea to their Imperial Waterfront Palace.

The Deputy Grand Vizier Pasha and the Commander-in-Chief Pasha, their excellencies, and the Gunpowder Works Superintendent—likewise with carriages, being imperial followers, came and returned to their places.

## Historical Context Notes

1. **Military Drill Commands:** This passage contains extensive Ottoman military drill terminology, demonstrating the adoption of European-style close-order drill in the post-Janissary Manṣûre army. Commands like "yarım ṣaġ örüş" (half-right wheel), "çift dizi" (double file), and "ṣaff-ı ḥarb" (battle line) reflect the new training regimen.

2. **140-Person Music Band:** The large military band (*mûzîḳa ṭaḳımı*) of 140 persons demonstrates the adoption of European military music traditions, replacing the traditional *mehter* bands.

3. **Fire Prevention Measures:** The requirement for neighborhood lamp-lit night watches (*îḳâd-ı ḳanâdîl*) for 20 days reflects the recurring problem of urban fires in Istanbul's densely-built wooden neighborhoods. The mention of the Beyoğlu fire (P3 RH) contextualizes this measure.

4. **Bârûtḫâne-i 'Âmire (Imperial Gunpowder Works):** Located near Yedikule, this was a critical military-industrial facility. The annual inspection by the Sultan personally demonstrates Mahmud II's hands-on approach to military modernization.

5. **Lehistanoz Köşkü:** "Lehistanoz" derives from "Lehistan" (Poland)—this pavilion near Yedikule was named after the Polish-style architecture or connection. The Sultan's overnight stay there during the gunpowder inspection shows his direct engagement with military matters.

6. **Hinṭov (Carriage):** The word comes from German/Hungarian "Landau" via *hintó*—the Sultan traveling by European-style carriage rather than on horseback represents another aspect of Mahmud II's modernization.
