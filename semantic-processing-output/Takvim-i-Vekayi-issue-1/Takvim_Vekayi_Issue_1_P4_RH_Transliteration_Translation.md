## Summary Translation (Flowing English)

### Section 1: Conclusion of Honors List (Continued from Previous Column)

...the Gate Steward of [the Commander-in-Chief] Pasha His Excellency, Yahya Bey, and the Chief Architect Abdülhalim Efendi—in the imperial presence, they attained the honor of receiving the medal.

And from ancient times, based on their witnessed loyalty and services: from the Greek nation, the former Deputy Governor of Moldavia, Stefanaki; and from the Armenian nation, Artin, who is the Imperial Mint Purchase Banker; and the Chief Gunpowder Maker Simon—also in the imperial presence, attaining this medal of favor, they found abundant favor.

Now, worthy of the glory of servitude and as an obligation of conscience and responsibility, loyalty is this: that the servants who have been honored with the noble medal of honor should [show] the continuation of loyalty from heart and soul, expending their capacity. And those who have not yet attained [the medal] should also not despair of kindness and sublime favor, but in their appointment to service should work with both hands of loyalty and zeal, making abundant effort to attain this honor, thereby becoming recipients of favor and attaining forgiveness.

### Section 2: Editorial Notice (Tenbîh) — On Robes of Honor vs. Medals

**NOTICE:** As the books of histories speak, from ancient times, in every state, when granting offices and appointing ranks and bestowing other honors, to the persons to be honored, according to their ranks, robes of honor (hil'at) are clothed upon them. And those persons also, if they are attendants at the council of the sultanate, at councils and on non-official days and at famous ceremonies, on witnessed days when people gather, they wear that robe of honor, and among their peers would display joy and pride.

Even in the eras of the Rightly-Guided Caliphs, their excellencies, to persons to be honored, they would give their own garments—that is, robes made of striped Yemeni fabric—they would clothe and bestow sewn kaftans. In summary, from the side of the sultanate, clothing someone in a robe of honor, remaining from ancient times, is called ṭırâz—a respected rule.

The all-over upper robe of honor, and the fur, and what they call the honor kaftan—this meaning and management of civil affairs, being a requirement, although it is a beautiful custom suitable to wisdom—our Padishah—may his victory be glorified—His Majesty's, from his desirable imperial demeanor, introducing good works of innovation—being of the presented category—instead of fur, bestowing jewels [i.e., jeweled medals]—truly, it is a kindness a thousand times superior to the ancient custom, and more abundant.

### Section 3: Prince Abdülmecid's Bed'-i 'Ulûm Ceremony (12 Rebiülâhir 1247 / September 20, 1831)

The noble offspring of His Imperial Majesty, the high-lineaged prince, the noble Abdülmecid—may he remain protected in the shadow of his father for a long time—Efendi His Excellency's [ceremony]. According to the ancient rule of the Sublime State, for the execution of the ceremony of beginning the learning of sciences (bed'-i 'ulûm), the imperial will of the Padishah was attached.

In Üsküdar, in Ibrahim Ağa Meadow, situated [there is] the Osman Pasha Pavilion. Having been repaired, in the open space in front of the imperial palace, [a pavilion] including eighteen columns and various ornaments and decorations—close to seven thousand purses [of money] having been spent—newly woven and constructed; at the end of the previous year, the completed imperial tent of encircling belt; and in its connection, newly made, the sky-marked imperial canopy; and the viziers and ulema and notables, and the Imperial and Victorious Army tents and pavilions; and in the Capital and the three lands, altogether for the school children, large tents; and for tent equipment, designated Victorious Army soldiers and employed engineers, with their inspections, ensuring geometric placement—everything was erected and fixed.

After several days—that is, the twelfth of Rebiülâhir and September's seventh [20th Gregorian], Monday—the Emperor of the World, our majesty-stationed Master, His Majesty, accompanied by the aforementioned prince, came from his boat with the imperial light-ceremony, arrived at Haydarpaşa Pier, placing his fortunate foot ashore and mounting his horse of majesty and grandeur.

With the aforementioned prince following, up to the mentioned palace, a two-sided line of straight formation, rank-adorning the station of salute—the rows of soldiers' columns—our majestic Master, when gladdening them with his gaze of favor, group by group, saying "My Padishah, long live!" The acclamation ceremony proceeded, and cannons were also fired. And the gathered school children also recited "Amen" with one voice. In that manner, with complete splendor, His Majesty honored the mentioned palace with his presence.

Afterwards, in the imperial court, to the left side of the imperial throne, [were] the Deputy Grand Vizier Pasha; and from the senior viziers who are presently residing in retirement in their waterfront mansions, Ebu Bekir Sıdkı Pasha of Morea, and Osman Pasha of Crete; and without office, residing at the Sublime Porte, Yusuf Muhlis Pasha of Serres, their excellencies; and the First and Second Imams of His Majesty—

## Historical Context Notes

1. **Non-Muslim Honorees:** The explicit mention of Greeks, Armenians, and possibly others receiving imperial honors alongside Muslims is significant. Stefanaki's service as deputy governor of Moldavia reflects the traditional Greek role in Ottoman Danubian principalities. Artin the banker and Simon the gunpowder maker represent Armenian technical expertise.

2. **Tenbîh (Editorial Notice):** This is a remarkable piece of early Ottoman journalism—an editorial explaining why the new medal system is superior to the traditional hil'at (robe of honor). The author traces the custom back to the Rightly-Guided Caliphs and Yemeni striped fabrics, then argues that Mahmud II's jeweled medals represent an innovation "a thousand times superior."

3. **Bed'-i 'Ulûm Ceremony:** This was the traditional Ottoman ceremony marking a prince's formal beginning of education. For Abdülmecid (born 1823, age 8 in 1831), this ceremony signaled his training as heir apparent. He would become Sultan in 1839 and issue the Gülhane Hatt-ı Hümayunu.

4. **Seven Thousand Purses:** A kîse (purse) contained 500 kuruş; 7,000 purses = 3,500,000 kuruş—an enormous sum spent on the ceremony pavilion.

5. **Retired Viziers:** The presence of former governors of Morea, Crete, and Serres—all Greek territories—at a ceremony in 1831, just after the Greek War of Independence (1821-1829), is politically significant. These were men who had governed now-lost territories.
